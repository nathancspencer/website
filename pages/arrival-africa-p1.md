---
title: Africa Part 1: Arrival
---

_Jan 2025_

“It’s just like Vietnam, except scarier, since everyone’s black” - my friend

I don’t know much about Africa. Well, I know a little bit about South Africa. My dad’s from there. He tells me that, during apartheid, it was nicer than Australia if you were white growing up. About the electric fences, maids and dogs. How you would pick out black labourers for home help by driving into the shanty towns and choosing whoever you felt the most sorry for. The one black kid in his school year not being able to join for the beach excursion because it was whites only. And how the fear of the collapse of this state drove my grandparents to migrate to Australia in the early 90s.

But that was a long time ago.

On my flight to Nairobi I was warned by my seatmate. Do not walk anywhere, you will be mugged. Even 50 meters down the street, take an Uber. Don’t go to the CBD. Stay in the expat zone - known as “Westlands”.  Oh, and if customs gives you trouble? Just hand over $10 USD and that should do the trick.

I arrived to Africa alone. Not by choice. I was originally supposed to be on the flight, a day before, with my friend from uni - but fortune decided that the budget airline I was flying would delay my first leg for “operational reasons”.

Getting off the flight the first thing that struck me was how polluted the city smelt. It smelt worse than Sydney did during the bushfire season of 2019.  But this was normal! I found out later that most of Africa burns their rubbish instead of putting it in landfill.

I could also breathe the inequality. Customs was more like a warehouse, a shed. There were no digital face scanners like in Australia. There was a mysterious guy in a trench coat who would manoeuvre people around, letting people skip in front of me as he saw fit. And I saw people hand over money to the customs agent, as my seatmate recommended.

After getting my baggage they make me scan the luggage through a machine aptly labelled “Kenya Revenue Authority” to check for valuables they might want to seize.

But nothing prepared me for the first steps walking out of the airport. There was a sea of African people, at least 100, trying to persuade me to take their taxi. There must have been a tip off that a large international flight had just arrived, and I was one of the first off the plane. I tried the Uber app, but it wouldn’t work, something to do with Apple Pay not being supported.

So into the den I went.

I’m being approached by 10 people at once all without valid badges, or cars. They’re tugging on my bag with force, they’re following me. I try to get prices, 2000, 2500 shillings to get to Westlands.

But I decided to ask for a badge, something legitimate. I figure there’s maybe some chance I get extorted if I go with someone I don’t trust.

One persistent lady showed me this screenshot someone has forwarded to her on WhatsApp of her badge, apparently. No one else has a badge.

For being white, a “wuzungu”, as they call it, there’s this pot of gold on my head which makes everyone want to ridiculously over quote me and talk in a way which I think Australians would find manipulative. Sure, I’ll quote you 3 times the market rate, but I’ll say I’ll make a special deal - no profit - since you are my friend and I want you to come back to Africa in the future. All while constantly smiling in a pretty creepy way, but which I’ve learnt is pretty normal.

I’m overwhelmed, I go back to the entrance which is a bit more protected, and a few of them follow me where I have to tell them very clearly no. I chat to this white lady who’s also booking a taxi who recommends I get airport security to take me to a valid one since I can’t get Uber to work.

Security takes me to this guy with a badge, who I remember having shook hands with security maybe 5 mins earlier. There’s something funny going on with this arrangement, I’m sure. But the badge looks legitimate enough, the price less so, but I figure what’s that in the risk of being extorted / kidnapped for more?

The guy tells me about the training course he has to do while we wait for his taxi. He calls some number from a list on Whatsapp. A yellow cab pops up, a driver comes out and this guy I’ve just met replaces him. There must be some car sharing arrangement? He makes me sit in the front, he seems to want to show me his list of prices (to show he isn’t making it up) in the glovebox while he is driving which scares me a little bit.

Anyways, we take the Chinese built motorway to the city and I share my location with my friends.

We arrive at the hotel and it is like how I would imagine Putin arrives at his home complex. It’s out of a dream. The tall gates open up, hand drawn by a couple Kenyans, and a team of 4 security guards approach the car with a massive flashlight. They check for bombs. The car is cleared and the driver drops me off at the entrance, I pay the fee, after trying to haggle but the guy has no change anyway (of course)  and I’m opened into something like airport security with a man with an AK-47 and I’m told to drop my bags and release metallic items. My friend meets me in the lobby.

I have arrived.
